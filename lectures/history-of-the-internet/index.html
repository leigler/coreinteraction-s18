The Mother of All Demos

  • MOAD was a live demonstration by Douglas Engelbart (an engineer and inventor known for founding the field of human-computer interaction) in Dec 1968. The demo introduced a complete collection of hardware and software (oN-Line System), and toured the audience through “windows, hypertext, graphics, efficient navigation and command input, video conferencing, the computer mouse, word processing, dynamic file linking (!), […] and collaborative edit[ing]”.
  • MOAD was the first closed system computer, demonstrating an operating system w/ multiple functions. It inspired the Apple Macintosh and Microsoft Windows.

ARPANET

  • ARPANET, a precursor to the internet was established in 1969. Funded largely by the United States Dept. for Defense, it was a coordinated attempt between multiple universities in California to transfer data through a method called “packet switching.”

ARPANET: A Timeline

HTML

  • Hypertext is digitally displayed text that allows the user to quickly navigate to related texts through “links”— perhaps comparable to Choose your Own Adventure books
  • HTML was introduced in an attempt to standardize the type of information that web browser software could interpret. As networks grew (slowly forming the internet we know today), there was a need for a standardized protocol of content interpretation.

The Hyperlink

  • Key to initial local hypertexts and subsequent HTML was the hyperlink— a method of navigating from resource to resource
  • The word hyperlink was coined in 1965 by Ted Nelson but inspired by an essay “As We May Think” written by Vannevar Bush in 1945 (an essay which essentially envisioned contemporary computers and the internet).
  • The hyperlink is vital not only to the internet, but to how we navigate information. Academic footnotes, colloquial references, and even memories are all arguably vague precursors to the hyperlink.

The URL

  • All webpages are assigned an IP address (000.000.000.000)— since these sequences are somewhat difficult to memorize, URL’s (Uniform Resource Locater) were introduced.
  • Like the hyperlink, the URL is vital to the success of the internet— it allows for the easy categorization of information for both humans and machines.
